About the Journal

Inspired by the story of Antaeus, the ancient giant-god of Greek mythology who was rendered powerless only by breaking contact with the earth, this journal welcomes to its pages those researching technological solutions that promise sustainability in the long term. Pro-nature and pro-environment in its fundamental orientation, this journal is committed to providing a première-quality space for research and review articles dealing with such phenomena as thermodynamic reversibility, negative entropy, direct applications of solar and wind energy, direct-use fuel cell, and any other approaches whose essence is profoundly innovative, economically attractive, environmentally appealing, and socially responsible.

Even though claims have been made to emulate nature, no modern technology truly emulates the science of nature. It has been quite the opposite: observations of nature have rarely been translated into pro-nature technology development. Today, some of the most important technological breakthroughs have been mere manifestations of the linearization of nature science: nature linearized by focusing only on its external features. Today, computers process information exactly opposite to how the human brain does. Turbines produce electrical energy while polluting the environment beyond repair, even as electric eels produce much higher-intensity electricity while cleaning the environment. Batteries store very little electricity while producing very toxic spent materials. Synthetic plastic materials look like natural plastic, yet their syntheses follow an exactly opposite path. Furthermore, synthetic plastics do not have a single positive impact on the environment, whereas natural plastic materials do not have a single negative impact. In medical science, every promise made at the onset of commercialization has proven to be opposite to what actually happened: witness Prozac, Vioxx, Viagra, etc. Nature, on the other hand, did not allow a single product to impact the long-term negatively. Even the deadliest venom has numerous beneficial effects in the long-term. This catalogue carries on in all directions: microwave cooking, fluorescent lighting, nuclear energy, cellular phones, refrigeration cycles to combustion cycles. In essence, nature continues to improve matters in its quality, as modern technologies continue to degrade the same into baser qualities.

Nature thrives on diversity and flexibility, gaining strength from heterogeneity, whereas the quest for homogeneity seems to motivate much of modern engineering. Nature is non-linear and inherently promotes multiplicity of solutions. Modern applied science, however, continues to define problems as linearly as possible, promoting singularity of solution, while particularly avoiding non-linear problems. Nature is inherently sustainable and promotes zero-waste, both in mass and energy. Engineering solutions today start with a safety factor while promoting an obsession with excess, hence waste. Nature is truly transient, never showing any exact repeatability or steady state. Engineering today is obsessed with standards and replicability, always seeking steady-state solutions.

There are thousands of science and engineering journals, but this is the only journal promoting the approach that breaks out of the traditional path of linearizing natural phenomena and accepting research that is inherently pro-nature. This journal offers the best hope of finally emulating nature rather than deviating from it.

Notes for Contributors

Journal of Nature Science and Sustainable Technology is a peer-reviewed journal that publishes four issues per year. The journal is owned and operated by Scientiae Naturalis Publishers and funded entirely by subscription revenue and author-optional publication services.

There is no charge to submit your article and have it published in the journal. Scientiae Naturalis offers a number of optional publication services that carry fees, but authors are not required to select these services, and the services will not be offered until after acceptance of the articles. These services include Open Access, color printing, article offprints, discounted print copies of the journal issue, and licenses for use of the published articles.

Authors will receive complimentary PDFs of their final articles upon publication. Authors may deposit and display the proofed versions of their articles in and on their personal non-commercial and affiliate non-commercial repositories and websites at any time. The same may be done with their final published articles only after a period of six months has elapsed from the day of publication.

Manuscript Submission Guidelines ▼

Articles should be submitted by e-mail directly to the Editor-in-Chief, Professor Stefano Menichetti, at procurement@scientiae-naturalis.org.

It is understood that articles submitted to the journal have not been submitted elsewhere for simultaneous publication consideration and have not been previously published, in whole or in part, unless the new article is an expansion of the original, in which case full transparency of the re-used portions must be provided. Articles cannot be retracted or withdrawn after they have been published.

Submission of an article must be accompanied by a signed Copyright Transfer and Disclosure Form. Submissions that do not include a signed Copyright Transfer and Disclosure Form will be treated as incomplete.

Manuscript Preparation ▼

Submissions should include the following items as separate files: a Title Page and an Anonymized Manuscript. The Title Page will remain separate throughout the peer-review process and should include a concise and informative title, all authors' complete names with academic degrees and affiliations, and a complete address for the corresponding author.

The manuscript should be typed in Times New Roman 12-point font. The text should be under the headings: Abstract, Keywords, Introduction, Methods, Results, Discussion, Acknowledgements, References, Tables, and Figures. Abstracts should not exceed 250 words. At least three key words should appear under the abstract.

MS Word and LaTeX are the preferred input programs. For LaTeX, Scientiae Naturalis requests use of the Scientiae macro (ARTICLE.STY, set at 5.5 inches, letter size, not A4). All illustrations, photographs, and charts should be referred to in the text as Figures and numbered consecutively with Arabic numerals. Figure resolution must be at least 300 dpi.

References Style ▼

The preferred style is APA for social sciences, Vancouver for biomedicine, AIP for physics, ACS for chemistry, and AMS for mathematics. Please use letter-size page setup. In-text references should be placed in square brackets on the line, e.g., [37].

Publication Ethics ▼

Scientiae Naturalis is committed to maintaining trust in the journal, the value of authorship, and the integrity of science. Plagiarism is prohibited. The unauthorized use or close imitation of another's language, text, data, ideas, or theories constitutes misconduct. All instances of proven plagiarism will be reported to the Publisher, the editorial office, the author's institute, and other governing bodies as necessary.

Authors should be sure to provide statements on: (1) sources of funding; (2) all potential conflicts of interest; (3) informed consent for all studies involving human participants; (4) human rights compliance; and (5) humane treatment of animals. These statements should be included in a section titled Ethical Compliance immediately preceding the References section.

Scientiae Naturalis and its editors follow the Codes of Conduct and Best Practice Guidelines set forth by the Committee on Publication Ethics (COPE): publicationethics.org/resources/code-conduct

Entropy Production Minimization in Biological Catalysis: Lessons for Sustainable Chemical Engineering
M. R. Jensen, A. Nakamura, L. Ferreira
Technical University of Denmark; Kyoto University, Japan; Universidade de Campinas, Brazil
This article examines how biological enzymatic systems achieve near-reversible catalysis at ambient temperatures and pressures, generating negligible entropy compared to their industrial analogs. Drawing on thermodynamic analysis of ATP synthase and cytochrome c oxidase, the authors develop a generalized framework for redesigning chemical reactors using principles of non-linear dynamics and homeostatic regulation. The study demonstrates that entropy production in biomimetic reactor prototypes was reduced by 38% compared to conventional designs under equivalent production conditions.
Received: March 2021 — Published: Q2 2021
JNSST — Vol. 13, No. 3
Solar Energy Harvesting Through Phyllotactic Arrangement of Photovoltaic Elements: A Biomimetic Approach
R. T. Abubakar, T. Watanabe, G. B. Novak
Ahmadu Bello University, Nigeria; Osaka University, Japan; Charles University, Czech Republic
Inspired by the Fibonacci-based phyllotactic leaf arrangement in plants, this study designed and tested a phyllotactic photovoltaic array capable of maximizing solar capture at all diurnal and seasonal angles without mechanical tracking systems. The proposed arrangement achieved 21.4% greater annual yield compared to conventional fixed flat-panel installations in temperate latitudes, while eliminating the energy overhead and maintenance cost of tracking hardware. The authors argue that phyllotaxis represents a superior organizational principle for distributed renewable energy systems.
Received: November 2020 — Published: Q3 2020
JNSST — Vol. 12, No. 4
Mycorrhizal Networks as a Model for Distributed Zero-Waste Nutrient Transport in Urban Agriculture
B. K. Srivastava, K. A. Mensah, C. Reyes
Indian Institute of Technology Delhi; University of Ghana; Universidad de Chile
The mycorrhizal network in forest ecosystems transfers nutrients and chemical signals between plant root systems with near-zero waste and high resilience. This paper proposes a reengineering of urban hydroponic agriculture loops around a distributed, decentralized exchange model derived from fungal mat topology. Laboratory simulations demonstrate nutrient utilization efficiencies above 94%, with system resilience to partial node failure significantly exceeding linear pipeline delivery models. Policy implications for urban food security are discussed in light of emerging smart-city infrastructure frameworks.
Received: June 2019 — Published: Q4 2019
JNSST — Vol. 11, No. 2
Direct-Use Hydrogen Fuel Cells Inspired by Hydrogenase Enzyme Architecture
H. O. Lindström, Y. Okonkwo, M. F. Rashid
KTH Royal Institute of Technology, Sweden; University of Ibadan, Nigeria; University of Dhaka, Bangladesh
Biological hydrogenase enzymes catalyze the reversible oxidation of molecular hydrogen with catalytic efficiencies exceeding 98%, without the platinum-group metals required by conventional fuel cell electrodes. This work reports the synthesis and electrochemical characterization of a nickel-iron active site mimic embedded in a conductive metal-organic framework scaffold. The resulting electrode material demonstrated hydrogen oxidation turnover frequencies comparable to Pt at neutral pH, with significantly improved tolerance to CO and sulfur poisoning. Life cycle assessment indicates a 70% reduction in embedded energy relative to Pt-based systems at equivalent power density.
Received: February 2019 — Published: Q2 2019
JNSST — Vol. 10, No. 1
Wind Turbine Blade Efficiency Enhancement Through Tubercle Geometry Derived from Humpback Whale Flippers
P. E. Dubois, N. Al-Rashidi, J.-H. Park
Université de Sherbrooke, Canada; Kuwait Institute for Scientific Research; Hanyang University, South Korea
The leading-edge tubercles of humpback whale (Megaptera novaeangliae) pectoral flippers have been documented to delay flow separation and reduce hydrodynamic drag at high angles of attack. This study applied sinusoidal leading-edge modifications to a NACA 63-415 wind turbine blade profile and evaluated aerodynamic performance across a range of tip-speed ratios in wind tunnel testing. Tubercled blades demonstrated a 17.5% increase in annual energy yield in low wind speed environments (below 7 m/s), with significantly reduced acoustic signature. The authors propose tubercle parametrization guidelines for practical deployment in small to medium wind installations.
Received: September 2017 — Published: Q1 2018
JNSST — Vol. 9, No. 3
Non-Linear Dynamics of Immune Response as a Template for Adaptive Pharmaceutical Drug Delivery
A. Wladis, S. M. Farouq Ali, D. L. Prior
Karolinska Institute, Sweden; University of Calgary, Canada; Veridity Environmental Technologies, Canada
The mammalian immune response exemplifies a highly non-linear, adaptive, multi-agent system capable of discriminating self from non-self, mounting calibrated defenses, and retaining immunological memory with extraordinary specificity. This paper argues that modern pharmacological regimens, in seeking standardized dosing and steady-state plasma concentrations, violate the fundamental non-linearity of biological signaling. The authors present a systems-biology-based adaptive dosing framework that modulates delivery timing and quantity in real time according to biomarker feedback, analogous to immune system operation. Preliminary simulations in oncological drug delivery indicate a 44% reduction in toxic side-effect probability without reduction in therapeutic efficacy.
